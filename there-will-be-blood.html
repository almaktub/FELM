Roll film
Capitalism and religion run the same con on the same town, and the movie watches to see which salesman blinks first.
10.0
Out of ten
Two of the greats, director and actor, hitting their absolute ceiling in the same film. That almost never happens. Here it happens for 158 minutes.
A 10 means I can't name a scene I'd touch, and I can't. The pace that feels slow on paper is the whole engine: every silence is Daniel calculating. If you want to dock it, the only honest complaint is that it's relentless, and that's not a flaw, that's the design. Nothing to take away, nothing missing. This is the score the scale exists for.
The Bench

Craft scorecard

Direction10
Anderson opens with 15 minutes of no dialogue and total command. He trusts the audience like almost no American director does: long takes, information withheld, violence arriving without warning. The film never explains Daniel. It just watches him until you understand.
Cinematography9.8
Robert Elswit won the Oscar for this and it wasn't close. Daylight that feels biblical, interiors lit like Rembrandt owed someone money, and the derrick fire at dusk, orange against a dying sky, is one of the most physically overwhelming images in modern film.
Acting10
Daniel Day-Lewis gives the performance other performances get measured against now. The voice, the gait, the warmth he can switch on like a salesman's lamp. And Paul Dano, playing twins and matching him scene for scene at 22 years old, never got enough credit for surviving this movie.
Writing9.7
Loosely carved out of Upton Sinclair's Oil! and better for the looseness. Dialogue is rationed like water in the desert, so when Daniel finally talks, every line is either a sale or a threat. Usually both.
Score & Sound10
Jonny Greenwood's strings sound like the land itself is anxious. It's horror scoring laid over a period drama, dissonant and wrong in exactly the right way, and it rewired what prestige films are allowed to sound like. The Academy ruled it ineligible on a technicality, which remains a crime.
Editing9.6
Dylan Tichenor holds shots three beats past comfortable and the discomfort is the point. Then the film jumps to 1927 in a single cut, skipping decades without ceremony, because Daniel's soul stopped changing long before his bank balance did.
Themes

What it's actually about

Daniel Plainview and Eli Sunday are the same animal in different costumes. One sells the ground, the other sells the sky, and both need an audience kneeling. The film's coldest joke is that the oilman and the preacher each force the other into a public humiliation ritual, baptism for Daniel, blasphemy for Eli, and each performs it convincingly because performing is all either of them ever does. America, the movie suggests, was built by exactly these two salesmen, working the same room.

Underneath the capitalism-versus-faith fight is something lonelier: a man for whom other people are either tools or competition. Daniel says it out loud once, in the film's only confession: he wants no one else to succeed. H.W. starts as a sweet face for buying land and becomes the one thing Daniel can't fully fake his way through. When he finally calls the boy a "bastard from a basket," he isn't telling H.W. the truth about his birth. He's telling himself a story that makes the loneliness feel like a choice.

And the oil itself is the theology. It's down there, it's nobody's, and whoever drinks first wins. The milkshake speech is remembered as a meme, but it's the film's entire worldview compressed into a children's metaphor: everything can be drained from underneath you, by a man smiling at you from across the room.

"I have a competition in me. I want no one else to succeed. I hate most people."
Daniel Plainview — There Will Be Blood
Standout Scenes

The frames that stay

TC 00:01:00
The Silent Prologue
Fifteen minutes, no dialogue. A man alone in a hole, breaking his leg, dragging himself across the desert on his back to file a silver claim. By the time anyone speaks you already know everything: this man will crawl through anything, and no one is coming to help him because he'd never ask. Most films spend an hour establishing less.
TC 01:10:00
The Derrick Fire
The well blows, H.W. loses his hearing, and Daniel stands in the orange glow saying "there's a whole ocean of oil under our feet" with his son deaf in a cabin behind him. Greenwood's drums turn it into a war scene. It's the moment the film shows you his exact exchange rate: one child's ears for an ocean, and he's already counting the barrels.
TC 01:58:00
The Baptism
Daniel needs a pipeline easement, the price is salvation, and Eli makes him scream "I've abandoned my child" in front of the congregation, slapping him like a man finally allowed to. Day-Lewis plays three layers at once: the performance for the room, the rage underneath, and somewhere at the bottom, horrifyingly, a flicker of the real thing. The best scene of the 2000s.
TC 02:30:00
The Bowling Alley
1927, a mansion, a private bowling alley, and the two salesmen meet one last time with the masks fully off. The milkshake, the chase, the pin. It's pitched at the edge of farce on purpose: this is what's left when a man finally wins everything. He's a creature in a basement, announcing he's finished. The funniest and bleakest ending of its decade, in the same breath.
Writing & Dialogue

On the page

The script understands that Daniel is a man who only speaks in pitches. His land speech to the Little Boston townsfolk ("I'm a family man") is word for word the same con as Eli's sermons, promises of schools, bread, irrigation, salvation. Anderson writes the two vocabularies, commerce and scripture, so they rhyme, and lets the audience notice on their own. And when Daniel finally stops selling, in the confession to his fake brother Henry, the honesty is more frightening than any threat in the film. The one true thing he ever says is that he hates most people.

The Honest Take

No notes withheld

Some 10s are warm. This one is a furnace you stand next to. It's not a film I'd recommend to everyone, and that has nothing to do with quality: it's two and a half hours inside a misanthrope, scored like a nightmare, ending in a basement. But every single department is operating at a level that has not been improved on since, and the central performance changed the job description for acting. I've seen it maybe six times and it has never once felt shorter or longer than it should. That's what a 10 is.

Rewatch Verdict
Rewatch It
It rewatches like scripture: you stop following the story and start studying the delivery. Every few years it reveals a new layer, usually in a scene you thought you had memorized.
