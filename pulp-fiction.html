Final Cut No. 2

Pulp Fiction

dir. Quentin Tarantino
1994 · Crime · 154 min
Roll film
Three crime stories told out of order, shuffled so that a redemption gets the last word.
9.3
Out of ten
Thirty years of imitators and not one of them figured out the trick: it's a hangout movie wearing a crime movie's jacket.
What keeps it from a 9.5 or higher: the pawn shop basement is shock doing the work that wit does everywhere else, and Tarantino casting himself to deliver his ugliest dialogue has aged like milk. What keeps it from dropping a point: the structure is still load-bearing, the dialogue still plays, and the diner ending still lands like a verdict. Films this imitated usually get worn smooth. This one didn't.
The Bench

Craft scorecard

Direction9.2
The confidence is the style. Tarantino lets scenes run eight minutes on talk alone, holds the trunk shot because it's his signature, and times every needle drop like a punchline. Almost nothing here is visually radical; what's radical is the nerve.
Cinematography8.8
Andrzej Sekula shoots on slow 50 ASA stock, which is why everything looks weirdly rich and saturated for a movie set in diners and apartments. The long Steadicam follow into Jack Rabbit Slim's is the flex. Serviceable everywhere else, deliberately so. The words are the visuals.
Acting9.6
Samuel L. Jackson gives one of the great American performances, a sermon machine slowly realizing he means it. Travolta plays Vincent half-asleep on purpose and made it a career resurrection. Willis underplays, Keitel cameos like royalty, and Uma Thurman built an icon out of a bob and a cigarette.
Writing9.8
The screenplay is the monument. Hitmen discuss foot massages with the seriousness of theologians, then the theology shows up for real. Every digression turns out to be the plot. People have it tattooed on themselves, which is its own review.
Score & Sound9.5
No composer, no original score, just crates of surf rock and soul deployed with sniper precision. Misirlou as an opening gun. "Girl, You'll Be a Woman Soon" scoring an overdose. It rewired how movies use pop music, for better and (in lesser hands) much worse.
Editing9.4
Sally Menke is the silent co-author. The chronology shuffle only works because every segment ends one beat after its strongest moment. A film this talky has no business moving this fast.
Themes

What it's actually about

Underneath the cool, it's a movie about whether grace exists. Jules and Vincent walk out of the same apartment, through the same miracle, and read it two different ways. Vincent calls it luck and goes back to work; Jules calls it God and quits. The film doesn't argue. It just arranges the timeline so you watch Vincent die in the middle and walk out grinning at the end, and lets you sit with what that means. The last scene of the movie is, chronologically, a man choosing to be better. That's the whole design.

It's also about talk as survival. Almost every scene is a negotiation: Jules talking Ringo down, the Wolf talking a corpse out of a garage, Butch talking Marsellus into mercy terms. In this world the fast gun matters less than the fast mouth, and the people who die are mostly the ones who run out of things to say.

"The truth is, you're the weak, and I'm the tyranny of evil men. But I'm tryin', Ringo. I'm tryin' real hard to be the shepherd."
Jules Winnfield — Pulp Fiction
Standout Scenes

The frames that stay

TC 00:14:00
Royale With Cheese → Ezekiel
Five minutes about European fast food, an elevator chat about foot massages, and then a door opens and the small talk becomes an execution with Bible verses. The scene taught a generation that menace is funnier and scarier when it arrives mid-conversation. Everything Tarantino is, good and bad, is in this one sequence.
TC 00:52:00
The Twist Contest
Two people who absolutely should not be alone together, dancing barefoot in a restaurant shaped like the 1950s. No plot advances. It's pure chemistry management, the tension of a date that isn't a date, and it became one of the most referenced scenes ever filmed without containing a single line anyone quotes.
TC 01:08:00
The Adrenaline Shot
Comedy and horror in a dead heat: a panicking Vincent, a drug dealer reading instructions off a medical book, a magic marker dot, and the hardest needle-related smash cut in cinema. Thurman sitting bolt upright is a jump scare played for a laugh, and somehow both work at full strength.
TC 01:28:00
The Gold Watch Monologue
Christopher Walken, one scene, talking to a child about a wristwatch and where it spent the war. It starts as solemn military legacy and ends somewhere unspeakable, delivered completely straight. It's the film in miniature: sentiment and filth welded together so tightly you laugh and feel something at the same time.
TC 02:20:00
The Diner Standoff
The opening scene resumes from the other side of the room, and the movie reveals it was a sermon the whole time. Jackson reciting Ezekiel for the second time, but slowly, actually thinking about the words, is the best acting of 1994. He talks two robbers out of dying and himself into a new life in the same breath.
Writing & Dialogue

On the page

The structural gag is famous, but the page-level trick is subtler: nobody in this movie talks about what the scene is about. The Ringo negotiation is about a wallet; it's actually about whether Jules meant his epiphany. The foot massage debate is about Tony Rocky Horror; it's actually Vincent being warned and not listening. Tarantino writes around the subject and lets the dread or the comedy leak in from the edges.

The flaws are also on the page. The Bonnie Situation leans on a slur Tarantino wrote for his own mouth, and it's the one stretch where the dialogue serves the writer's self-image instead of the story. The film survives it. It shouldn't have to.

The Honest Take

No notes withheld

It's not the best film of the 90s, but it might be the most 90s film of the 90s, and it's the one that rearranged the furniture. Every crime movie with chatty hitmen, every nonlinear indie, every soundtrack album that outsold the movie: this is the patient zero. Influence isn't quality, but in this case the quality is also just there, on screen, scene after scene.

Where it dips: the pawn shop sequence trades the film's wit for pure transgression, and the Butch chapter takes its time finding a pulse before Walken hands it one. Where it soars: every minute Jackson is on screen. If Parasite is a film where every department points the same direction, this is a film where one department (the screenplay) points and everything else salutes.

Rewatch Verdict
Rewatch It
It's comfort food that happens to be a landmark. The chronology means the rewatch is a different film: you know Vincent's future during every scene he's alive, and the diner ending gets heavier each time.
