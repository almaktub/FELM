Spirited
Away

dir. Hayao Miyazaki
2001 · Animation · 125 min
Roll film
A ten-year-old's name is the only thing standing between her and disappearing, and the movie treats that like the highest possible stakes. It's right.
10.0
Out of ten
The rare 10 where the mechanics resist explanation. It runs on dream logic that never once breaks its own spell, and no one else has ever pulled that off at this scale.
The case against is always the same: the plot wanders, things happen without setup, the rules are never stated. All true, and all of it is the achievement. It plays by the logic of an actual dream and stays coherent anyway, because the emotional throughline (a child learning to hold onto herself) never wavers for a frame. Films with airtight plots are common. Films with airtight feelings are not.
The Bench

Craft scorecard

Direction10
Miyazaki famously started without a finished script, drawing the story forward board by board, and somehow that produced his most complete film. He gives weight to things no one else would animate: shoes being tapped on, a sandwich eaten while crying. The quiet between events ("ma," he calls it) is the masterstroke.
Cinematography9.9
Hand-drawn composition and color doing the work a camera crew does elsewhere. The bathhouse glows like a furnace of lanterns, the spirit world gets more saturated as the human world fades, and the flooded railway, sky and sea fused into one plane, is one of the most beautiful images in any medium.
Acting9.5
For animation this means character acting in the drawings, and it's flawless: Chihiro's body language matures across the film from noodle-limbed sulking to planted, certain stillness. Rumi Hiiragi voices her without a trace of precociousness. Even the soot sprites have legible inner lives.
Writing9.6
No villain, which almost no screenwriter has the nerve to attempt. Yubaba is an employer, No-Face is loneliness with a body, and every conflict resolves through work, manners, or memory instead of victory. The script trusts a child audience with ambiguity that most adult films won't risk.
Score & Sound10
Joe Hisaishi's "One Summer's Day" might be the single most emotionally efficient piece of film music ever written: six notes in and something in your chest gives way. The score knows when to vanish too, leaving just water, wind, and train rhythm.
Editing9.5
The pacing breaks every Western rule: the climax is quiet, the action peak happens early, and the film's most important stretch is a silent train ride where nothing happens. Every rule it breaks, it replaces with rhythm. Nothing drags, nothing rushes.
Themes

What it's actually about

Names are the currency of the spirit world, and that's the film's deepest idea. Yubaba doesn't imprison workers with chains; she takes their names and lets them forget themselves into servitude. Haku has been a river spirit so long unnamed that he can't find his way home. Chihiro survives because she keeps one syllable of herself hidden. Anyone who has moved between countries or languages knows exactly what this is: the quiet danger of becoming whatever the new place calls you.

It's also a film about appetite. The parents eat themselves into pigs within ten minutes. No-Face discovers that the bathhouse will love anything that produces gold, so he produces gold and swallows everything that approaches, getting hungrier with every meal. The film's answer to consumption isn't a lecture, it's Chihiro, the one person in the building who wants nothing from anyone, repeatedly refusing payment. Generosity as immunity.

And underneath everything: work. Chihiro saves herself not through destiny or power but by demanding a job and doing it well, scrubbing the floor, bathing the stink spirit nobody else will touch. Miyazaki's radical message to children is that competence is a form of magic, maybe the only reliable one.

"Once you've met someone, you never really forget them. It just takes a while for your memories to return."
Zeniba — Spirited Away
Standout Scenes

The frames that stay

TC 00:10:00
The Parents Become Pigs
The single scariest scene in any children's film, and it's just two adults eating. The wet sound design, the unhurried camera, the moment Chihiro pulls her father's shoulder and a pig's face turns around. No transformation sequence, no music sting. Just gone. It teaches you in one cut that this world doesn't negotiate.
TC 00:55:00
The Stink Spirit
A reeking mountain of sludge no one will serve, assigned to the new girl as a hazing. Chihiro finds the thorn in its side, the whole bathhouse pulls together, and out comes an ancient river god, polluted into unrecognizability, leaving behind a heap of dumped bicycles and trash. An environmental thesis delivered as a triumphant set piece, and the film never says the word pollution once.
TC 01:20:00
No-Face Comes Apart
The shy spirit Chihiro let in from the rain becomes the bathhouse's best customer and worst nightmare, swallowing staff and spraying gold, swelling with every transaction. It's the film's horror movie stretch and its sharpest satire at once: an institution happily feeding the thing that's eating it, as long as the gold keeps coming.
TC 01:42:00
The Train Ride
Chihiro, No-Face, and two transformed companions ride a half-submerged train to nowhere, surrounded by translucent passengers, while Hisaishi's "The Sixth Station" plays. Nothing happens. It's the emotional summit of the film anyway, the moment the adventure goes quiet and becomes memory while you're still inside it. Nobody else would have the nerve to put this scene here. It's the best scene Miyazaki ever made.
TC 01:55:00
"Your Name Is Kohaku River"
Falling through open sky, Chihiro remembers drowning as a small child and being carried to shore by a river. She returns Haku's name to him and his dragon scales burst away like shedding glass. The film's whole theology in one moment: remembering someone truly is the act that frees them.
Writing & Dialogue

On the page

The dialogue is functional on purpose; the writing lives in the rules of the world, which are never explained and always consistent. Eat the food, belong to the place. Lose your name, lose yourself. Help freely, and help returns from strange directions. Zeniba's line about memory is the closest thing to a stated thesis, and even that arrives as grandmotherly small talk over tea. Compare it to the Western fantasy of the same era, drowning in prophecy exposition, and the restraint looks even more radical.

The Honest Take

No notes withheld

I've tried to find the flaw and the candidates all dissolve under inspection. The wandering middle is the dream working. The unexplained rules are the world's integrity. The quiet ending, no battle, no confrontation, just a guess about pigs and a walk back through a tunnel, is the bravest choice in the film. It won the Oscar, outgrossed Titanic in Japan, and still feels handmade and private, like something one person dreamed and was somehow able to show you. Animation has produced other masterpieces. It has never produced another one of these.

Rewatch Verdict
Rewatch It
It changes with you. As a kid it's an adventure, in your twenties it's about work, later it's about everything you've forgotten and the people who carried you to shore. The film stays still; you move past it at different angles.
