Final Cut No. 1

Parasite

dir. Bong Joon-ho
2019 · Thriller · 132 min
Roll film
A home invasion where nobody breaks in. They get invited through the front door, one fake credential at a time.
9.5
Out of ten
The rare film where every department is doing the same job at the same time: pointing at the line between floors and asking who drew it.
To hit a flat 10 it would need to resist naming its own symbols, and it doesn't quite (more on the rock later). To drop a point it would have to fumble the tonal handbrake turn at the midpoint, and it never comes close. This is as controlled as filmmaking gets while still feeling alive.
The Bench

Craft scorecard

Direction9.8
Bong shifts from caper comedy to horror to tragedy without a single gear grinding. Watch how he blocks the two families: Parks framed in open space and glass, Kims in corners, under tables, below sightlines. The camera knows the class system before anyone says a word.
Cinematography9.4
Hong Kyung-pyo shoots verticality like a language. Sunlight is something the rich own and the poor borrow. The flood sequence at night, lit like the city itself is drowning the Kims on purpose, is the best-looking 10 minutes of 2019.
Acting9.5
Song Kang-ho does more with a slow change of expression at a garden party than most actors do with a monologue. Park So-dam's "Jessica, only child, Illinois, Chicago" is instant canon. Cho Yeo-jeong makes the gullible rich wife warm instead of a punchline, which is harder.
Writing9.7
A script that splits in half at a doorbell ring and pays off every single thing it sets up: the rock, the peaches, the morse code, the smell. The structure is the story.
Score & Sound9.0
Jung Jae-il scores a con job with baroque strings, which is the joke: the Kims' hustle presented as chamber music for polite society. And the Italian pop song over the birthday party is sound design as cruelty.
Editing9.6
The infiltration montage is a heist sequence built from tutoring sessions and laundry tips. Later, the cross-cutting between the Parks driving home and the Kims scrambling to erase themselves is pure tension engineering.
Themes

What it's actually about

Class as architecture. The Kims live in a semi-basement, the Parks live up a hill, and a man nobody remembers lives below everything. The film never needs a speech about inequality because the staircases are the speech. Every character is exactly as high above sea level as their bank account allows, and when the rain comes, the water obeys the same map.

The title is a question with no safe answer. The Kims feed on the Parks' money, sure. But the Parks can't drive, can't cook, can't manage their own children, can't grieve without staff. Pull either family out and the other collapses. Bong isn't saying the poor are leeches. He's saying the whole arrangement is one organism, and it's sick.

And then there's the smell. The one thing Ki-taek can't fake, can't study for, can't perform his way out of. The Parks never insult the Kims, not once. They're nice the whole way through. The cruelty is a wrinkled nose in the back seat of a car, and the film argues that this, not malice, is what eventually puts a knife in someone's chest.

Standout Scenes

The frames that stay

TC 00:33:00
The Infiltration Montage
Peach fuzz as a chemical weapon, a rehearsed script about tuberculosis, and a family of four conning their way into one household with the rhythm of an Ocean's movie. It's the most fun the film ever lets you have, which is the trap. You're rooting for them right up until the movie asks what you were rooting for.
TC 01:20:00
The Long Walk Down
The Kims flee the house in the rain and the camera just keeps descending. Stairs, then more stairs, then tunnels, then a street that has become a river of sewage. One unbroken trip from the Parks' hilltop to a flooded toilet geysering filth. No dialogue needed. It's the film's whole thesis as geography.
TC 01:51:00
The Garden Party
A child's birthday staged like a colonial pageant, an impromptu "performance" with a real knife, and Ki-taek watching Mr. Park hold his nose while reaching for car keys under a dying man. The kill isn't planned. It's years of accumulated humiliation discharging in two seconds. Song Kang-ho plays the moment like his body decides before his brain does.
TC 02:01:00
The Letter
Ki-woo's plan to buy the house and free his father, shot like a flash-forward, revealed as a daydream from the same semi-basement window where the film began. Bong gives you the happy ending and takes it back in one cut. The cruelest part is how reasonable the dream sounds while you're inside it.
Writing & Dialogue

On the page

The dialogue does its best work sideways. Nobody discusses class; they discuss smells, "crossing the line," whether the dead housekeeper found a new job. Mr. Park's highest compliment for an employee is that they never cross the line, and he repeats it like a man praising a fence. Meanwhile the Kims' patter is performance all the way down: Ki-jung inventing an art-therapy diagnosis on the spot, Ki-woo rebranding himself as "Kevin." The script's running gag, "you know what kind of plan never fails? No plan," lands as comedy in the gym and as a eulogy by the end.

One nitpick, since this section exists to be honest: the scholar's rock. Ki-woo literally calls it "so metaphorical," and the film winks at its own symbolism a couple of times. Bong is clearly in on it, mocking the kind of student who'd say that. But a wink is still a wink, and the film is otherwise so disciplined that you notice.

The Honest Take

No notes withheld

This deserved every award it dragged home. Not because it's important, but because it's airtight entertainment that happens to be important. It's funnier than most comedies and tenser than most thrillers, and it pulls off the single hardest thing in movies: a twist at the midpoint that makes the film better instead of just different.

What doesn't work? Almost nothing structural. The basement man's backstory asks for a little generosity, and the symbolism occasionally signs its own name. That's the whole list. Some films you respect, some films you enjoy. This one doesn't accept the distinction.

Rewatch Verdict
Rewatch It
First viewing is for the trapdoor. The second is for the breadcrumbs: every payoff is planted in plain sight, and watching the setups land is a different movie. Arguably a better one.
